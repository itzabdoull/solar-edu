Lesson 1

What is solar energy?

Solar energy is energy from the sun. Earth receives this energy as electromagnetic radiation, including visible light and other wavelengths. Solar technologies capture this energy either as heat or as electricity.

In everyday conversation, people often use “solar” to mean photovoltaic electricity. Photovoltaic systems convert sunlight directly into electrical energy without moving mechanical parts in the panel itself.

Two main solar pathways

  • Solar thermal: sunlight is used to create heat.
  • Solar photovoltaic: sunlight is converted into electricity.

This website focuses mainly on photovoltaic systems because they are common in homes, schools, clinics, offices, and mini-grid applications.

Lesson 2

Essential terms every beginner should know

Power (kW)

Power is the rate at which electricity is being used or produced at a given moment. A 1 kW appliance is drawing 1 kilowatt while it is operating.

Energy (kWh)

Energy is power used over time. If a 1 kW load runs for 3 hours, it uses 3 kWh of energy. Solar production and electricity bills are usually discussed in kWh.

Peak sun hours

This is a simplified way to express how much solar energy a site receives in a day. It helps estimate how much electricity a solar array can generate.

Lesson 3

How a solar panel makes electricity

Solar panels are built from many photovoltaic cells. When photons from sunlight strike these cells, they transfer energy to electrons inside the semiconductor material. If the cell structure is designed correctly, those electrons begin to move in a useful direction, producing direct current electricity.

The panel does not “store” sunlight. It converts incoming light into electrical output in real time. Storage happens only if a battery is included elsewhere in the system.

Important beginner takeaway

A panel by itself is not enough to run most homes or classrooms. A complete solar system also needs electrical balance-of-system components such as wiring, mounting, protection, and usually an inverter.

Lesson 4

Basic types of solar power systems

Grid-tied

Connected to the utility network. Often lower cost, but usually cannot operate during outages without special design.

Off-grid

Operates independently of the utility. Requires careful battery sizing and load management.

Hybrid

Combines solar, batteries, and often grid or generator support. Common where power supply is unreliable.

Portable or small-scale

Used for small devices, lighting, pumps, security systems, or field applications.

Lesson 5

Common myths beginners should avoid

Myth: More heat means better solar performance

Panels need sunlight, but excessive heat can reduce efficiency. Strong sun is helpful; high cell temperature can be harmful.

Myth: Solar instantly removes all electricity bills

That depends on system size, usage pattern, storage capacity, and the relationship between the solar system and the grid.

Myth: Solar systems are maintenance-free forever

They are relatively low-maintenance, but cleaning, battery care, inspections, and inverter monitoring still matter.
